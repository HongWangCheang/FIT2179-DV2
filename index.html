About This Visualisation

Australia is one of the world's seventeen megadiverse countries, home to an estimated 600,000 species — many found nowhere else on Earth. Yet decades of habitat clearing, invasive species, altered fire regimes, and climate change have pushed more than 1,900 species onto the national threatened list under the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation (EPBC) Act.

This atlas brings together data from the EPBC Act listings, the Atlas of Living Australia (ALA), the Murray-Darling Basin wetland surveys, and national fire monitoring datasets to tell the story of Australia's biodiversity crisis — and the conservation efforts working against it.

Each section pairs an interactive chart with a focused insight, letting you explore the data at your own pace. Scroll through to move from the big picture — how many species, which states, which groups — down to the pressures driving decline and the actions that offer hope for recovery.

The Scale of the Crisis

Which Australian wildlife species are most at risk? How does the threat distribution vary across Australia's states and territories?

Insight The crisis does not sit within one part of nature. Plants dominate the list, but animals are deeply affected too: birds, mammals, reptiles, frogs, and fish all appear among the most threatened groups. This shows that biodiversity loss is spreading across entire ecosystems, not just affecting a few isolated species.
Insight The map shows that the threatened species crisis is not evenly spread across Australia. Western Australia and New South Wales stand out as major pressure points, where rich biodiversity overlaps with expanding land use, habitat clearing, and development. These darker regions tell a story of ecosystems carrying both high natural value and heavy conservation risk.
01

Section Summary · Scale of the Crisis

Australia has more than 1,900 species listed as threatened under the EPBC Act, spanning every major group in nature. Plants dominate the count, but mammals, birds, reptiles, frogs, and fish are all heavily represented — this is not an isolated crisis confined to one part of the natural world. Geographically, Western Australia and New South Wales carry the heaviest burden, where exceptional biodiversity collides with decades of land clearing, urban expansion, and habitat fragmentation.

Land clearing is not a background stressor — it is the primary driver pushing individual species toward the edge.

Trends & Biological Pressures

Tracking the accelerating growth in EPBC-listed threatened species over time, and the leading biological forces — invasive predators, disease, competition — driving that decline.

Insight Both animal and plant listings have climbed steadily since the EPBC Act came into force in 2000, with the total number of threatened species more than doubling over two decades of continuous monitoring.
🔬 Invasive Species Spotlight Invasive

European Rabbit

Oryctolagus cuniculus

📅 Introduced to Australia in 1859 — now one of the nation's most destructive feral animals
🌿 Strips vegetation and causes severe soil erosion, degrading habitat for native species
⚠️ Linked to the decline of over 300 threatened species through competition and habitat damage

Key driver of biological pressure · Listed among Australia's worst invasive pests

📷 Image source: lafeber.com
Insight Extinction risk is not caused by one single pressure. Invasive species, changed fire regimes, habitat clearing, climate stress, and human disturbance overlap like layers of pressure around the same ecosystems. Together, these forces create a chain reaction, pushing hundreds of EPBC-listed species closer to long-term decline.
Insight As monitoring expands, Australia is not simply finding more biodiversity — it is uncovering more species already under pressure. The rise in ALA records and EPBC listings suggests that better surveying is revealing hidden risk, while the taxonomic breakdown shows this crisis spreading across plants, birds, fish, mammals, reptiles, and frogs.
03

Section Summary · Trends & Biological Pressures

Both animal and plant listings have climbed without pause since 2000, with the total number of threatened species more than doubling over two decades. As biodiversity monitoring expands, Australia is not simply finding more wildlife — it is uncovering more species already under pressure, with ALA records and EPBC listings rising in step. Behind the numbers sit overlapping biological forces: invasive predators, altered fire regimes, and vegetation clearing each affect hundreds of listed species, compounding one another to drive decline far faster than any single threat could alone.

Ecosystems Under Threat

Comparing threatened ecological communities across Australia's states and territories, and mapping the scale of planned and unplanned fire events from 2016 to 2021.

Insight New South Wales stands out as the most intense hotspot, with Queensland and Western Australia also carrying serious pressure. These red zones suggest that long-term habitat clearing and fragmentation are not only threatening individual species, but pushing whole ecosystems toward endangered status.
📷 Black Summer 2019–20 Australia's most catastrophic fire season burned over 18.6 million hectares, killed an estimated 3 billion animals, and pushed dozens of species closer to extinction. 📷 Image source: The Guardian
Insight The Northern Territory and Queensland appear as major fire zones, while large circles reveal where unplanned fires dominate. These patterns suggest that fire is not only frequent, but also difficult to control, placing already vulnerable ecosystems under repeated stress.
04

Section Summary · Ecosystems Under Threat

The biodiversity crisis extends beyond individual species to entire habitats. New South Wales and Queensland host the highest concentrations of Critically Endangered ecological communities — habitats so degraded that whole ecosystems are approaching collapse. Fire compounds the pressure: between 2016 and 2021, Northern Territory and Queensland recorded the most intense fire activity, with large unplanned fires — the most ecologically disruptive type — repeatedly burning through high-biodiversity regions.

🏞️ NSW leads in CE communities 🔥 NT & QLD fire hotspots 💥 Unplanned fires dominate

Wetland Health & Biodiversity Monitoring

Exploring wetland fill levels and waterbird populations across Murray-Darling Basin survey sites, alongside ALA biodiversity monitoring records compared with EPBC threatened species listing increases over time.

Insight This map shows Australia’s wetlands as a connected network of ecological refuges. The dots stretch across the states, but the largest wetlands stand out in the north and Murray–Darling Basin, where water systems support major bird habitats. Their uneven distribution highlights why protecting key wetland sites is essential for migratory and endemic waterbirds.
💧 Wetland Spotlight — Coongie Lakes A remote, flood-driven wetland in outback South Australia, Coongie Lakes is one of Australia's most ecologically intact inland wetland systems. Listed under the Ramsar Convention since 1987, it supports mass waterbird breeding events with hundreds of thousands of birds arriving after flood pulses. 📷 Image source: bobcaddell.com
Insight When wetland fill levels rise, waterbird abundance often increases, revealing how healthy, well-watered sites become refuges for large bird populations. The pattern suggests that water management decisions can quickly shape whether wetlands support life or remain under ecological stress.
05

Section Summary · Wetland Health & Biodiversity Monitoring

Australia's 67 Ramsar-listed wetlands are unevenly distributed across the continent, with the largest sites concentrated in the Murray-Darling Basin and Northern Territory — regions that provide critical refuge for migratory and endemic waterbirds. Survey data from the Basin reveals a direct relationship: sites with higher water fill levels consistently support larger waterbird populations, demonstrating that water management decisions have immediate ecological consequences for some of Australia's most threatened wildlife.

💧 66 Ramsar wetland sites 🦆 Water levels drive bird counts 🗺 Murray-Darling Basin focus

Conservation Actions & Species Recovery

Ranking the conservation strategies that benefit the most threatened species, and tracking the number of species successfully removed from the threatened list over time as a measure of conservation progress.

Insight Predator and pest management benefits the greatest number of threatened species, followed by habitat protection and weed control — pointing to where conservation investment delivers the highest return for wildlife recovery.
🔬 Species Spotlight Recovered

Humpback Whale

Megaptera novaeangliae

✅ Removed from the EPBC threatened species list in 2022 — one of Australia's most celebrated conservation recoveries
📈 Population rebounded from near extinction after the global ban on commercial whaling in the 1980s
🌊 Now estimated at over 40,000 individuals migrating along Australia's east and west coasts each year

EPBC Act · Delisted 2022 · A landmark success for marine conservation

📷 Image source: antarctica.gov.au
Insight This chart tells a story of slow and uneven recovery. Most years, only a few species are removed from the threatened list, while rare spikes show moments when formal reviews or conservation success become visible. The long gaps between peaks suggest that recovery is still fragile and far slower than the growth of the threatened species crisis.
06

Section Summary · Conservation Actions & Species Recovery

Conservation works — but slowly. Predator and pest management benefits the greatest number of threatened species, followed by habitat protection and weed control, pointing to where investment delivers the highest return. Yet removals from the threatened list remain rare and uneven: most years see only a handful of species recover, with occasional peaks when formal reviews are completed. The pace of recovery is far below the rate at which new species are added — a gap that underscores the scale of the challenge still ahead.
