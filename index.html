The Scale of the Crisis

Which Australian wildlife species are most at risk? How does the threat distribution vary across Australia's states and territories?

Insight Mammals and birds account for the largest share of Australia's threatened species, but reptiles, frogs, and fish are also heavily represented — revealing the broad cross-taxonomic reach of the biodiversity crisis.
Insight Western Australia and New South Wales carry the heaviest burden of threatened species, reflecting their exceptional biodiversity and the intense land-use pressures concentrated along the eastern seaboard.
Insight As ALA biodiversity monitoring records continue to grow, EPBC threatened listings rise in parallel — suggesting that expanded surveying is uncovering species at risk faster than conservation efforts can recover them.

Trends & Biological Pressures

Tracking the accelerating growth in EPBC-listed threatened species over time, and the leading biological forces — invasive predators, disease, competition — driving that decline.

Insight Both animal and plant listings have climbed steadily since the EPBC Act came into force in 2000, with the total number of threatened species more than doubling over two decades of continuous monitoring.
Insight Invasive animals, altered fire regimes, and vegetation clearing are the dominant biological pressures — each affecting hundreds of listed species and compounding each other to accelerate the extinction crisis.

Ecosystems Under Threat

Comparing threatened ecological communities across Australia's states and territories, and mapping the scale of planned and unplanned fire events from 2016 to 2021.

Insight Critically Endangered ecological communities are concentrated in New South Wales and Queensland, where decades of clearing and fragmentation have pushed entire habitat types to the brink of collapse.
Insight Northern Territory and Queensland recorded the most intense fire activity between 2016 and 2021. Circle size highlights where unplanned fires — the most ecologically disruptive — overlap with high-biodiversity regions.

Wetland Health & Biodiversity Monitoring

Exploring wetland fill levels and waterbird populations across Murray-Darling Basin survey sites, alongside ALA biodiversity monitoring records compared with EPBC threatened species listing increases over time.

Insight Australia's 66 Ramsar-listed wetlands are spread across the continent, with the largest sites concentrated in the Murray-Darling Basin and Northern Territory, providing critical refuge for migratory and endemic waterbirds.
Insight Wetland fill level is a strong predictor of waterbird abundance — survey sites with higher water levels consistently support larger bird populations, showing how water management decisions directly drive ecological outcomes.

06

Conservation Actions & Species Recovery

Ranking the conservation strategies that benefit the most threatened species, and tracking the number of species successfully removed from the threatened list over time as a measure of conservation progress.

Insight Predator and pest management benefits the greatest number of threatened species, followed by habitat protection and weed control — pointing to where conservation investment delivers the highest return for wildlife recovery.
Insight Removals from the threatened list are rare and uneven, peaking in years when formal reviews are completed. The overall rate of recovery remains far below the pace at which new species are added, underscoring the scale of the challenge ahead.
